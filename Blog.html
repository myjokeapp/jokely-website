Joke Writing Mechanics

Setup. Misdirection. Payoff. Repeat.

Short answer: the setup lies, the punchline corrects it.

How do I overcome writer's block when starting a new set?

ü¶ä
Writer's block is fear with a name tag. Say whatever's true and dumb first ‚Äî collect premises, then sharpen. Use quick voice notes: your brain is terrible at saving punchlines unless you do.

One knockout, optional tags.
#MainPunch
ü¶ä
Start with one punch that slaps. Add tags only when they hit as hard. Most newbies add filler that slows the laugh. Track which tags live and which die ‚Äî analytics don't lie.

Order matters. Your memory falters; your structure shouldn't.

First and last rule.
#Opener
ü¶ä
Open with your best joke. End with your best closer. Between them, place two to three solid bits that flow in energy. Don‚Äôt test radical new material in the first 30 seconds ‚Äî the room doesn't owe you a warm-up.

Stage Rule
ü¶ä

Stage Performance Analysis

Record. Compare. Improve.
What should I do immediately after I "bomb" on stage?
Don't sulk. Collect data.
Field Notes
ü¶ä
Write down three things: the moment the room checked out, what you changed from your script, and any outside factors (mics, noise, crowd). Bombing is feedback ‚Äî harvest it. Later, listen to the recording and isolate the exact syllable where it went south.
How do I know if my energy level is right?
Match energy to material.
#Energy
ü¶ä
If you look bored, the crowd is bored. If you're frantic, they're confused. Record sets ‚Äî pick the energy where the laughs hit hardest. Tag jokes with #HighEnergy or #Deadpan to build flow that breathes.

Crowd Work & Improv

Crowd work is a tool, not a hobby.
When should I do crowd work vs stick to my material?
Use it to recover or amplify.

ü¶ä
If your written stuff's landing, don't gamble. Use crowd work when the room's cold or after a bomb to reset energy. Keep safe scripts: quick questions that create a clear riff. Build a catalog of reliable crowd prompts in your notes.

Short, universal, riffable.
#GoTo
ü¶ä
"What do you do for work?"‚Äîsafe, easy to riff, and often yields gold. Avoid personal trauma or religion. After the show, tag the successful interactions so you can reuse them precisely.

ü¶ä

ü¶ä

Callbacks & Themes

Callbacks are cheat codes when done right.
What is a callback and when should I use it?
Return to a joke later for compounded laughs.
Theme
ü¶ä
A callback references an earlier joke in the set to amplify the laugh. Use callbacks as anchors ‚Äî they make the set feel like a finished story. Don't force them; plant seeds early and harvest them late.

Less is more.
Rule
ü¶ä
One or two solid callbacks in a short set is enough. Overusing callbacks turns your show into an inside joke the audience doesn't share. Use them to reward attention, not to punish missed lines.

ü¶ä
Onstage: smile, reset with safe crowd work, and move on‚Äîdon't apologize or explain. Offstage: write down the exact moment and reason, then test a revised version next time. Bombs refine your taste faster than success.

ü¶ä
Don't argue. Make the audience your ally with a quick, funny line that signals you're steering the ship. If someone gets aggressive, remove them politely or call the host. Your job is to keep the room safe for laughter.
